# Transformer Architecture

## History

The Transformer architecture was introduced in June 2017, originally focused on translation research. It later gave rise to a series of influential models:

- **June 2018**: [GPT](https://cdn.openai.com/research-covers/language-unsupervised/language_understanding_paper.pdf) — the first pretrained Transformer model, fine-tuned for various NLP tasks and achieving state-of-the-art results.
- **October 2018**: [BERT](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.04805) — another large pretrained model designed to produce better sentence representations.
- **February 2019**: [GPT-2](https://cdn.openai.com/better-language-models/language_models_are_unsupervised_multitask_learners.pdf) — an improved, larger version of GPT, initially withheld from public release due to ethical concerns.
- **October 2019**: [T5](https://huggingface.co/papers/1910.10683) — a sequence-to-sequence Transformer implementation focused on multi-task learning.
- **May 2020**: [GPT-3](https://huggingface.co/papers/2005.14165) — a much larger GPT-2 that performs well across many tasks without fine-tuning (known as zero-shot learning).
- **January 2022**: [InstructGPT](https://huggingface.co/papers/2203.02155) — a GPT-3 variant trained with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) to better follow instructions.
- **January 2023**: [Llama](https://huggingface.co/papers/2302.13971) — Meta's large language model capable of generating text in multiple languages.
- **March 2023**: [Mistral](https://huggingface.co/papers/2310.06825) — a 7B parameter model that outperforms Llama 2 13B on multiple benchmarks, using Grouped-Query Attention for faster inference and Sliding Window Attention for arbitrary-length sequences.
- **May 2024**: [Gemma 2](https://huggingface.co/papers/2408.00118) — a family of lightweight open-source models from Google (2B–27B) that leverage Knowledge Distillation, enabling small models to match ones 2–3× their size.
- **November 2024**: [SmolLM2](https://huggingface.co/papers/2502.02737) — extremely small language models (135M–1.7B) designed for mobile and edge computing scenarios.

[See more Transformer history](https://huggingface.co/learn/llm-course/en/chapter1/4#a-bit-of-transformer-history)

Notable models fall into roughly 3 categories:
- GPT-like, also known as auto-regressive Transformer models
- BERT-like, also known as auto-encoding Transformer models
- T5-like, also known as sequence-to-sequence Transformer models

## Training

All Transformer models are trained as language models on large amounts of raw text using a **self-supervised** approach.

**Self-supervised learning** is a training paradigm where the objective is automatically derived from the model's inputs, requiring no manually labeled data.

### Transfer Learning

A pretrained general-purpose model is then adapted to a specific task through **transfer learning** or **fine-tuning**. This process switches to **supervised** learning, which requires human-annotated labels.

Why not just train from scratch on the target task? There are several practical reasons:

1. **Inherited language understanding**: The pretrained model has seen massive amounts of text and already has a statistical understanding of language. Fine-tuning builds directly on this foundation without having to relearn "what language is."
2. **Far less data required**: Training from scratch demands enormous datasets; fine-tuning achieves good results with a small amount of task-specific data.
3. **Much lower time and cost**: Full pretraining can take weeks; fine-tuning typically takes only a few hours.

In short: **let someone else spend the big money laying the foundation — you just build the room you need on top of it.**

### Causal Language Modeling

Predicts the next word in a sentence given the preceding n words. The output depends only on past and present inputs, not future ones.

```
My         --> name
My name    --> is
My name is --> David
```

### Masked Language Modeling

Predicts words that have been masked out in a sentence.

```
My [MASK] is David.
   (name)
```

## Transformers Are Big Models

A common strategy for improving model performance is to scale up model size and pretraining data volume. Training on massive datasets consumes enormous amounts of time, compute resources, and even has environmental impact.

Imagine if every research team, student group, or company had to train a model from scratch — the cumulative cost would be staggering and unnecessary. This is why sharing pretrained models matters: building and fine-tuning on top of existing weights reduces overall compute costs and carbon footprint.

## Attention Layers

The defining feature of Transformer models is the **Attention Layer**, which is where the 2017 paper title ["Attention Is All You Need"](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) comes from.

The role of the attention layer is to tell the model which other words in the sentence to focus on when processing a given word.

**Example**: Translating "You like this course" into French

- When translating "like," the model must pay attention to "You," because French verbs conjugate based on the subject
- When translating "this," the model must pay attention to "course," because French articles agree with the gender of the noun
- The remaining words in the sentence are not relevant to these two translations

This mechanism allows the model to understand the relationships between words, rather than treating each word in isolation.

## Architecture vs. Checkpoint

When reading documentation or papers, you'll often encounter these three terms — they mean different things:

- **Architecture**: The model's skeleton — defines each layer and its computation logic.
- **Checkpoint**: The saved weight file after training a particular architecture.
- **Model**: A general term that can refer to either an architecture or a checkpoint; it's imprecise.

**Example**: BERT is an Architecture; `bert-base-cased` is a Checkpoint released by Google. Saying "the BERT model" or "the `bert-base-cased` model" are both acceptable, but they carry slightly different meanings.

## Encoder

- Purpose: "understanding" — transforms input into meaningful feature representations
- **Bidirectional attention**: each token can attend to both preceding and following context, giving the model a deeper understanding of overall semantics
- A common pretraining approach is to "corrupt" sentences (e.g., masking random words) and have the model reconstruct them

**BERT in detail**

BERT is the most representative Encoder model, with two pretraining objectives:

1. **MLM (Masked Language Modeling)**: randomly masks a portion of tokens and has the model predict them from context. This is the key mechanism that enables BERT's bidirectional understanding.
2. **NSP (Next Sentence Prediction)**: presents the model with two sentences and asks it to determine whether they are consecutive. Half are genuine adjacent sentences; half are random pairings, helping the model understand inter-sentence relationships.

BERT also introduces two special tokens:
- `[CLS]`: prepended to every input; the final `[CLS]` vector represents the semantics of the whole sentence and is commonly used for classification tasks
- `[SEP]`: separates two sentences, marking sentence boundaries for the model

## Decoder

- Pretraining is typically framed around predicting the next word in a sentence
- Unidirectional attention (can only look backward, not forward)
- The primary architecture for text generation

### Modern LLMs

Almost all modern large language models use the Decoder architecture, often with hundreds of billions of parameters. Training proceeds in two stages:

1. **Pretraining**: predicting the next token on large text corpora, building the model's foundational understanding of language
2. **Instruction Tuning**: fine-tuning the model to follow instructions and produce useful responses

---

## Encoder-Decoder

- Also called **sequence-to-sequence models** — well-suited for transforming one form of text into another
- Pretraining typically involves reconstructing a sentence that has been "corrupted" in some way, such as masking random words
- Ideal for tasks that require generating new sentences from a given input, such as translation, summarization, and generative question answering

## How to Choose the Right Model Type

Ask yourself three questions:
1. What kind of understanding does your task require? (Bidirectional or unidirectional)
2. Are you generating new text or analyzing existing text?
3. Do you need to convert one sequence into another?

Mapped to specific tasks:

| Task | Recommended Architecture | Representative Models |
|------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Text classification (sentiment, topic) | Encoder | BERT, RoBERTa |
| Named Entity Recognition (NER) | Encoder | BERT, RoBERTa |
| Extractive QA (extracting answers from text) | Encoder | BERT, RoBERTa |
| Text generation, conversational AI | Decoder | GPT, LLaMA |
| Translation | Encoder-Decoder | T5, BART, Marian |
| Summarization | Encoder-Decoder | BART, T5 |
| Generative QA | Encoder-Decoder or Decoder | T5, GPT |
