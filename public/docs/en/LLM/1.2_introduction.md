# Introduction to NLP and LLMs

## NLP

Natural Language Processing is a branch of linguistics and machine learning focused on understanding everything related to human language.

### Common NLP Tasks

- **Classifying whole sentences**
- **Classifying each word in a sentence**
- **Generating text content**
- **Extracting an answer from a text**
- **Generating a new sentence from an input text**

> NLP isn't limited to text — it also tackles complex challenges in speech recognition and computer vision.

### Transformers Beyond NLP

The Transformer architecture was originally designed for text, but has since been applied to solve problems in speech and vision as well:

**Speech — Whisper**
OpenAI's speech recognition model. It uses an Encoder-Decoder architecture and was trained on 680,000 hours of audio data.
It doesn't require fine-tuning for specific languages — it can recognize multiple languages zero-shot out of the box.
The pipeline is: raw audio → Log-Mel spectrogram → Encoder → Decoder auto-regressively outputs text.

**Vision — ViT (Vision Transformer)**
Images can't be fed directly into a Transformer, so ViT splits an image into small patches (analogous to tokens in text) and passes those patches through a Transformer Encoder.
Instead of processing pixels, it processes "image vocabulary" — conceptually almost identical to a text task.

### Why Language Is Challenging

Due to the complexity of language, there are still many fundamental challenges, including **understanding ambiguity**, **cultural context**, sarcasm, and humor.
Language models can be trained at scale on diverse datasets, but in many complex situations their comprehension still falls short of human-level understanding.

---

## LLM

A Large Language Model (LLM) is an AI model trained on massive amounts of text data.
LLMs can understand and generate text that resembles human language, recognize various linguistic patterns, and perform a wide range of language tasks — all without being trained on a specific task. This has fundamentally transformed the field of natural language processing. Architectures like GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) and Llama have redefined what's possible in language processing.

### Key Characteristics

- **Scale**: They contain millions, billions, or even hundreds of billions of parameters
- **General capabilities**: They can perform multiple tasks without task-specific training
- **In-context learning**: Models can learn from examples provided directly in the prompt
- **Emergent abilities**: As these models scale up, they exhibit capabilities that were neither explicitly programmed nor anticipated

### Limitations

- **Hallucinations**: Models can confidently generate incorrect information
- **Lack of true understanding**: They lack genuine understanding of the world and operate purely on statistical patterns
- **Bias**: They can reproduce biases present in their training data or inputs
- **Context Window**: Models have a limited context window (though this is steadily improving)
- **Computational resources**: Models require significant computational power

---

## Bias and Limitations

These models are powerful, but they come with inherent limitations. To pre-train on large amounts of data, researchers typically scrape everything they can find — the best content on the internet, and the worst. This makes models prone to generating content that is sexist, racist, or otherwise biased. Even fine-tuning cannot eliminate this deeply ingrained **bias**.

**A concrete example that's uncomfortable but illustrative**

Using BERT's fill-mask to predict "job occupations":

```python
# "This man works as a [MASK]."
# → lawyer, carpenter, doctor, waiter, mechanic

# "This woman works as a [MASK]."
# → nurse, waitress, teacher, maid, prostitute
```

Note: BERT was trained on English Wikipedia and BookCorpus — relatively neutral sources, not random internet garbage — and the bias is still there.

This illustrates an important point: **bias doesn't only come from bad data. Language itself carries social bias, and what models learn is the statistical patterns of language.** Fine-tuning can change output behavior, but it cannot change the bias baked into the model's weights. This is a risk that must be kept in mind whenever these models are used in production settings.
